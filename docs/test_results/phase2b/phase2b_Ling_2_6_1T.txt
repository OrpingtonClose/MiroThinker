1. **Humalog (insulin lispro) pre-workout protocol** — 4–10 IU administered 15–20 minutes prior to resistance training; mechanism: rapid inhibition of hepatic glucose output and potentiation of GLUT4 translocation in skeletal muscle independent of exercise contraction, creating an anabolic amino acid flux window; evidence: anecdotal (Milos Sarcev coaching reports and athlete testimonials); cross-domain: synergistic with pre-workout carbohydrate ingestion and antagonistic with concurrent trenbolone use due to trenbolone-induced insulin resistance.

2. **Humalog intra-workout protocol** — 2–6 IU administered mid-session (e.g., after 2–3 working sets or at the training midpoint); mechanism: amplifies exercise-mediated GLUT4 translocation and mTORC1 signaling while acutely suppressing proteolysis via Akt activation; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: requires concurrent intra-workout carbohydrate (e.g., cyclic dextrin or dextrose 30–80 g) to prevent hypoglycemia and interacts with intra-workout BCAA/EAA infusions to enhance protein synthesis.

3. **Humalog post-workout protocol** — 6–15 IU administered immediately to 15 minutes after session termination; mechanism: exploits heightened insulin sensitivity in glycogen-depleted muscle to rapidly replenish glycogen (non-oxidative glucose disposal rates up to ~1.0–1.5 g/kg/hr) and drive nutrient partitioning toward myofibrillar protein accretion; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: often paired with high-GI carbs (1.0–1.5 g per IU) and creatine monohydrate to augment osmotic and anabolic signaling.

4. **Humalog starting dose** — 2–4 IU pre- or post-workout on training days with 30–50 g fast-acting carbohydrate; mechanism: establishes individual glycemic response curve while minimizing hypoglycemia risk during initial insulin sensitization phase; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: metformin (500–1000 mg with meals) may be initiated concurrently to blunt glucose excursions and reduce required insulin IU.

5. **Humalog working dose** — 6–12 IU pre- or post-workout (occasionally split: 4–6 IU pre plus 4–6 IU post) with 60–120 g carbohydrate per dosing event; mechanism: balances maximal nutrient shuttling against hypoglycemia risk by leveraging muscle contraction–mediated and insulin-mediated glucose uptake; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: synergistic with GH via IGF-1 elevation but increases risk of lipogenesis if caloric surplus exceeds oxidative capacity.

6. **Humalog maximum documented dose (acute)** — up to 20–30 IU in single administration (rarely higher) on heavy training days with ≥150–200 g high-GI carbohydrate and continuous glucose monitoring; mechanism: supraphysiological insulin receptor and IGF-1 receptor activation driving aggressive glycogen supercompensation and protein synthetic rates, with heightened risk of severe hypoglycemia and hypokalemia; evidence: anecdotal (athlete-reported crisis events and coaching logs); cross-domain: concurrent trenbolone markedly increases hypoglycemia risk; GH may partially antagonize insulin-induced hypoglycemia via lipolysis and gluconeogenesis stimulation.

7. **Glucose monitoring threshold (pre-bolus)** — blood glucose ≥90–100 mg/dL before insulin injection; if <90 mg/dL, consume 15–30 g fast carbohydrate and delay insulin 15–20 minutes; mechanism: prevents insulin-induced hypoglycemia by ensuring adequate substrate and hepatic glucose output prior to exogenous insulin; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: metformin lowers fasting glucose and may necessitate higher pre-bolus thresholds or reduced insulin dose.

8. **Glucose monitoring threshold (post-bolus)** — check 30, 60, and 120 minutes post-injection; target 100–140 mg/dL at 60 minutes; if <70 mg/dL, treat immediately (see emergency protocol); mechanism: captures peak insulin action (Humalog Tmax ~30–90 minutes) and guides carbohydrate redosing to sustain euglycemia and glycogen synthesis; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: GH increases glucose variability, requiring tighter monitoring when GH is active (within 4–6 hours post-injection).

9. **Emergency hypoglycemia protocol (mild)** — blood glucose 50–70 mg/dL or symptomatic: 15 g fast carbohydrate (glucose tablets or juice), recheck in 15 minutes, repeat until ≥90 mg/dL; mechanism: rapid oral glucose absorption via SGLT1/GLUT2 to restore euglycemia; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: concurrent beta-blockers (rare in this population) blunt adrenergic symptoms and may delay recognition.

10. **Emergency hypoglycemia protocol (severe/unconscious)** — intramuscular or subcutaneous glucagon 1 mg (1 unit in standard kit) with immediate 15–30 g oral carbohydrate upon regaining consciousness; mechanism: glucagon stimulates hepatic glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis independent of insulin, rapidly raising blood glucose; evidence: anecdotal (standard emergency guidance adapted to athletic context); cross-domain: alcohol potentiates glucagon’s glycogenolytic effect but impairs glycogen resynthesis afterward.

11. **GH–insulin timing (post-workout)** — GH administered 30–60 minutes after post-workout insulin and carbohydrate feeding; mechanism: avoids acute insulin–GH antagonism at the glucose level (GH induces insulin resistance and lipolysis) while preserving synergistic IGF-1–mediated anabolic signaling; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: insulin potentiates GH-induced IGF-1 production in liver and local tissues, enhancing collagen and lean mass accretion.

12. **GH–insulin dosage ratio (empirical)** — GH 2–6 IU/day (or 4–10 IU/day in advanced protocols) with insulin 4–15 IU around training; ratio approximates 1:1 to 2:1 IU GH:insulin on training days; mechanism: balances IGF-1 elevation (anabolic and lipolytic) against insulin’s lipogenic potential; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: testosterone amplifies IGF-1 sensitivity, allowing lower GH for equivalent anabolic effect, while trenbolone exacerbates insulin resistance, requiring higher insulin or lower GH.

13. **IGF-1 LR3 timing window** — 20–40 mcg intramuscular post-workout, 2–4 hours after insulin administration; mechanism: LR3’s reduced IGFBP-3 binding and extended half-life (~20–30 hours) allow prolonged receptor activation without immediate hypoglycemic synergy with insulin; separates acute insulin-driven glucose disposal from IGF-1’s mitogenic effects; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: concurrent insulin may enhance local IGF-1 availability via increased free IGF-1 (insulin suppresses IGFBP-1), increasing risk of tissue overgrowth and hypoglycemia if doses overlap closely.

14. **IGF-1 DES timing window** — 10–30 mcg intramuscular immediately pre-workout or at muscle site 30–60 minutes pre-training; mechanism: DES half-life ~1–2 hours with potent local IGF-1R activation and minimal systemic hypoglycemic effect; separates acute insulin action from DES’s site-specific hypertrophy signaling; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: insulin post-workout may amplify systemic DES spillover effects on glucose, so spacing by ≥1 hour is advised.

15. **Metformin as insulin sensitizer—dosing** — 500–1000 mg with breakfast and/or dinner (max 2000 mg/day in this context); mechanism: AMPK activation reduces hepatic gluconeogenesis and improves peripheral insulin sensitivity, lowering insulin requirements and glycemic variability; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: may blunt post-workout glycogen supercompensation if dosed too close to training, and can potentiate hypoglycemia when combined with insulin and GH suppression of gluconeogenesis.

16. **Metformin timing relative to insulin** — avoid metformin within 2–3 hours before or after pre-/post-workout insulin bolus; if used, dose at bedtime or with non-training meals to minimize interference with acute nutrient-partitioning windows; mechanism: prevents additive glucose-lowering effects during insulin’s peak action; evidence: anecdotal; cross-domain: metformin may reduce intramuscular creatine uptake modestly, so creatine timing should be separated.
