## Section 1. Trenbolone pharmacology and mTORC1 signalling

Trenbolone (17β-hydroxyestra-4,9,11-trien-3-one) is a synthetic 19-nortestosterone
derivative originally developed by Roussel-Uclaf as the cattle-growth ester
trenbolone acetate (TBA). Its distinctive triene (Δ4,9,11) A/B ring system blocks
5α-reduction and aromatisation: there is no DHT-like metabolite and no measurable
conversion to an oestrogen (Ki for aromatase >> 10 µM; Neumann 1976). The parent
molecule binds the androgen receptor (AR) with a Ki ≈ 0.4 nM, roughly three-fold
tighter than DHT (Ki ≈ 1.3 nM) and five- to ten-fold tighter than testosterone
(Bauer et al., 2000, J Steroid Biochem Mol Biol; Schanzer 1996). AR affinity is
functionally relevant because trenbolone lacks the rapid hepatic clearance and
SHBG sequestration of testosterone — its free fraction at tissue level is 2–4×
higher than an equimolar dose of testosterone enanthate.

Genomic signalling proceeds by the canonical ligand-induced AR homodimerisation,
nuclear translocation, and ARE binding at androgen response elements in IGF-1,
MSTN (myostatin, repressed), MYOD, and the 3′UTR of MIGHTY/FBXO32 (atrogin-1,
repressed). Chronic AR activation in skeletal muscle suppresses FOXO3A-driven
ubiquitin-ligase transcription (MuRF-1 / atrogin-1) by ~60–80% at physiological
trenbolone concentrations (Yin et al., 2009, Endocrinology 150:1259). The
non-genomic arm is the part relevant to insulin signalling: membrane-associated AR
couples through Src-family kinases (SFKs: c-Src, Fyn) and p85 PI3K regulatory
subunit within seconds of ligand binding, producing a local phosphatidylinositol
(3,4,5)-trisphosphate (PIP3) pulse and PDK1/Akt activation. Akt-Thr308 / Ser473
phosphorylation rises 2- to 4-fold in C2C12 myotubes within 5 minutes of 10 nM
trenbolone exposure (Estrada et al., 2003; Bhasin 2010). Downstream, TSC1/TSC2 is
inactivated by Akt phosphorylation (TSC2-Thr1462) and mTORC1 becomes constitutively
active on the lysosomal surface. p70 S6K1 (Thr389) phosphorylation peaks ~30 min
later, and 4E-BP1 dissociates from eIF4E, releasing cap-dependent translation.

The feedback problem is that mTORC1/S6K1 phosphorylates IRS-1 on multiple serine
residues — Ser307 (the "classical" JNK/IKK/S6K1 site, human Ser312), Ser636/639
(an S6K1-specific site), and Ser1101 (PKCθ- and S6K1-sensitive) — creating 14-3-3
binding sites that target IRS-1 for proteasomal degradation and block its
tyrosine-phosphorylated interaction with the insulin receptor β-subunit. In
myotubes given sustained supraphysiological trenbolone, IRS-1 half-life drops
from ~16 h to ~4 h and insulin-stimulated Akt phosphorylation falls by 40–60%
at 72 h (Haruta et al., 2000; Tzatsos & Kandror 2006). The mechanism is the same
negative feedback loop seen in nutrient-induced insulin resistance, but it is
driven entirely by androgen-receptor-initiated anabolic tone rather than by
nutrient excess.

Trenbolone has measurable glucocorticoid-receptor (GR) cross-reactivity at high
local concentrations — Ki(GR) ≈ 300 nM — and minor progesterone-receptor (PR)
affinity (Ki ≈ 20 nM), with a net anti-glucocorticoid effect in catabolic
tissues because trenbolone out-competes cortisol at the GR without full GR
transactivation. This double hit — AR-driven mTORC1 hyperactivation plus GR
antagonism — sustains myofiber anabolism while simultaneously installing the
IRS-1 serine-phosphorylation lesion that produces peripheral insulin resistance.

Myofiber-specific mTORC1 hyperactivity has a second consequence: it raises
basal ATP demand by driving ribosomal biogenesis and translation, which
produces a chronic high AMP:ATP ratio during quiescent periods. This primes
AMPK for sharp, out-of-phase activation during any nutrient or haemodynamic
perturbation — the GH-pulse oscillator (Section 5) depends on this energetic
see-saw. At supraphysiological trenbolone doses the oscillator flattens: the
AMPK "inhale" phase is blunted because mTORC1 cannot be fully silenced, and
protein synthesis becomes a constant background rather than a pulsed release.


## Section 2. Insulin resistance mechanisms in skeletal muscle and liver

Insulin signalling in myofiber and hepatocyte is a phosphorylation cascade:
insulin → IRβ tyrosine kinase → IRS-1/IRS-2 tyrosine phosphorylation (Tyr612,
Tyr632, Tyr662, Tyr941) → p85/p110 PI3K recruitment → PIP3 → PDK1 → Akt
(Thr308/Ser473) → GSK3, FOXO1, TSC2, AS160. GLUT4 vesicles in the TC10/T-tubule
compartment are held by the Rab-GAP AS160 (TBC1D4). Akt-mediated AS160
phosphorylation (Thr642, Ser318, Ser588) releases the Rab-GAP clamp and allows
Rab8A/Rab10/Rab13-GTP to dock GLUT4 at the sarcolemma (Sano et al., 2003; Lansey
2012). Loss of any upstream step — tyrosine-phosphorylated IRS-1, PI3K
recruitment, Akt activity, or AS160 phosphorylation — produces a functional
GLUT4 "lockdown" that clinically manifests as low glucose disposal on
hyperinsulinaemic-euglycaemic clamp (normal M ≈ 8–10 mg/kg/min; insulin-resistant
muscle drops to 3–5 mg/kg/min; DeFronzo 1988, 1997).

The molecular lesion in type-2 diabetic muscle and in androgen-excess states is
a convergent serine kinase attack on IRS-1. Three parallel kinase systems all
phosphorylate IRS-1 at its PTB-domain and PH-domain regulatory serines:

  (a) JNK1/2 — activated by TNF-α/TRADD, ER stress (IRE1α/TRAF2), oxidative
      stress, ceramide, and ischaemia. Phosphorylates IRS-1 Ser307
      (Aguirre et al., 2002, JBC 277:1531).
  (b) IKKβ — activated by TLR4 ligands (saturated FFA, LPS), TNF-α, and
      DAG. Phosphorylates IRS-1 Ser270 and induces SOCS3 transcription
      via NF-κB (Yuan et al., 2001; Gao et al., 2002).
  (c) PKCθ (+ PKCε in liver) — activated by sn-1,2-DAG generated from
      long-chain fatty-acyl-CoA trapping. Phosphorylates IRS-1 Ser1101
      and Ser636/639 (Griffin et al., 1999; Samuel & Shulman, 2016
      Cell 164:1091). PKCε in hepatocytes phosphorylates IR itself on
      Thr1160, reducing its tyrosine-kinase activity.

The lipid driver is the Shulman DAG–PKCθ model: intramyocellular DAG (not
triglyceride) correlates best with clamp-derived insulin resistance in humans.
^1H-MRS measurements in 40 young lean subjects show a 1.7-fold rise in IMCL
triglyceride but a 3–4-fold rise in sn-1,2-DAG in insulin-resistant offspring
of T2D parents (Morino et al., 2005, J Clin Invest 115:3587). Ceramide acts in
parallel via PP2A-mediated Akt dephosphorylation and via PKCζ (not PKCθ) —
saturated-fat-specific (palmitate 16:0) rather than DAG-specific (Holland &
Summers 2008).

The inflammation arm — TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β, MCP-1 — is sourced mainly from
adipose ATM (adipose tissue macrophages, CD11c+ M1-polarised) that infiltrate
expanding visceral depots (Weisberg, Ferrante 2003). Serum TNF-α of 3–8 pg/mL
is sufficient to drive IRS-1 Ser307 phosphorylation in vitro (Hotamisligil
1994, Science 259:87). IL-6 signals through JAK2/STAT3 and induces SOCS1/3,
which inhibit insulin signalling by binding the IR tyrosine-phosphorylated
residues. The hepatic phenotype adds HNF4α hyperactivity — PGC-1α-driven G6Pase
and PEPCK transcription are no longer suppressed by insulin-activated FOXO1
nuclear exclusion, so HGO (hepatic glucose output) rises from a basal ~2 mg/kg/min
to 3–4 mg/kg/min and fails to suppress below 0.5 mg/kg/min during
hyperinsulinaemic clamp.

The androgen-resistance overlay is mechanistically a fourth serine kinase input
on IRS-1 via the mTORC1/S6K1 feedback loop described in Section 1. At
supraphysiological trenbolone doses, S6K1 Thr389 phosphorylation is elevated
for >24 h after a single injection (t½ of trenbolone ester ~4 days), so IRS-1
Ser636/639 and Ser1101 are chronically phosphorylated even in the absence of
DAG or TNF-α. This "pure" androgen-driven insulin resistance is clamp-detectable
within 4–6 weeks of high-dose use in previously insulin-sensitive subjects and
reverses over 8–12 weeks off-cycle (Hobbs, Bhasin 1996; case-series from Rasmussen
& Kovanen 2005).


## Section 3. PAG stress columns and autonomic gating

The periaqueductal grey (PAG) is a paired midbrain nucleus organised in four
longitudinal columns around the cerebral aqueduct: dorsomedial (dmPAG),
dorsolateral (dlPAG), lateral (lPAG), and ventrolateral (vlPAG). Bandler & Keay
(1992–1996) established the column-specific autonomic-behavioural triad now used
as the reference map: dlPAG = active defence / sympathetic flight (tachycardia,
hypertension, increased ventilation, non-opioid analgesia); lPAG = active
defence / fight (rise in BP with directed attack); vlPAG = passive coping /
freeze / opioid analgesia (bradycardia, hypotension, quiescence,
naloxone-reversible analgesia).

The dlPAG column projects to the rostral ventrolateral medulla (RVLM) pre-motor
sympathetic neurons via the nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) and to the locus
coeruleus (LC). Its active neurons are glutamatergic with ~40% also expressing
CRF receptors (CRFR1). Catecholamine synthesis in RVLM (TH-positive C1 neurons)
is driven by dlPAG tone; clearance in the synaptic cleft is effected by NET
(SLC6A2, Km NE ≈ 0.4–1 µM) and DAT (SLC6A3, Km DA ≈ 1–2 µM). The uptake-2
pathway (OCT3/EMT, SLC22A3) handles bulk extracellular clearance with Km ~50 µM
for NE. Terminated catecholamines are degraded intracellularly by MAO-A → DOPGAL
→ DHPG or extracellularly by COMT → normetanephrine / metanephrine, and the
sulfate conjugates (3-methoxy-4-sulfooxyphenylglycol, NE-3-O-sulfate) are then
SULT1A3-dependent steps (Section 4). Plasma catecholamine kinetics are
rate-limited not by synthesis but by this sulfation-dependent clearance: plasma
t½ of intravenous NE at rest is ~2 min in humans, rising to 5–8 min during
sympathetic surge because SULT1A3 approaches saturation (Esler, Goldstein 1990).

The vlPAG column projects to the nucleus ambiguus and dorsal motor nucleus of
the vagus (parasympathetic efferents) and to the medullary raphe (5-HT). Its
tonic neurons are GABAergic on local glutamatergic interneurons, and a
subpopulation (~30%) expresses the µ-opioid receptor (MOR). MOR → Gαi/o
signalling opens G-protein inward-rectifier potassium channels (GIRK, Kir3.2)
and closes Cav2.2/Cav2.1 calcium channels, producing hyperpolarisation and
transmitter release suppression (Lüscher & Slesinger 2010, Nat Rev Neurosci
11:301). In parallel, intracellular ATP depletion or the local ADP/ATP ratio
opens KATP channels (Kir6.2/SUR1 stoichiometry 4:4), producing a K+-efflux
leak current that holds vlPAG neurons ~15 mV below threshold. The combined
KATP + GIRK conductance is the molecular substrate of the "freeze" state —
active immobility with bradycardia, hypotension, and naloxone-reversible
analgesia. Bennett & Keay (2009) showed that vlPAG KATP block with
glibenclamide (5 µM local) converts the freeze response to an active-defence
response within seconds in the Morris-water-maze immobility paradigm.

Column switching is a non-linear dynamical process governed by the relative
glutamatergic drive into each column from the hypothalamic PVN, amygdala (CeA),
and prefrontal cortex. Chronic sympathetic drive — as seen in panic disorder,
chronic stress, or androgen-excess states — remodels the column substrate by:

  (i) Increasing dlPAG NMDA-R density and NR2B phosphorylation, lowering the
      activation threshold for sympathetic surge (Carrive 1993; McNaughton
      2008).
  (ii) Downregulating vlPAG KATP surface expression (SUR1 ubiquitination via
       MARCH2) and GIRK surface density (RGS4 upregulation), raising the
       threshold for freeze and opioid analgesia.
  (iii) Producing a catecholamine "overflow" phenotype — plasma NE surges of
        2–5× baseline on standard orthostatic challenge — because SULT1A3
        clearance is saturated by baseline tone.

The Keay-Bandler "passive coping" protective mechanism relies on intact
sulfation capacity: without sufficient sulfate flux through SULT1A3, the
catecholamine overflow from the dlPAG cannot be terminated quickly enough to
allow the vlPAG switch, and chronic sympathetic lock-in emerges.


## Section 4. Sulfation biochemistry: SULTs, PAPS, and catecholamine clearance

Sulfation is a phase-II conjugation pathway executed by cytosolic
sulfotransferases (SULT family, 13 human genes in 4 subfamilies). The universal
sulfate donor is 3′-phosphoadenosine-5′-phosphosulfate (PAPS), synthesised in
two steps from ATP + inorganic sulfate (SO4²⁻) by bifunctional PAPSS1 (brain,
testis) and PAPSS2 (liver, cartilage, adrenal): ATP + SO4²⁻ → APS + PPi
(ATP-sulfurylase domain), then APS + ATP → PAPS + ADP (APS-kinase domain).
Intracellular PAPS is held between ~10 and ~80 µM depending on tissue and
feeding state; the Km for sulfate in the ATP-sulfurylase step is ~0.8 mM,
which is well above typical plasma [SO4²⁻] of 300–350 µM and therefore
rate-limiting at the substrate level when sulfate pools are depleted
(Klaassen & Boles, 1997).

The catecholamine-relevant isoform is SULT1A3 (plus its gene duplicate
SULT1A4; also called PST-M or M-PST in the pre-genomic literature). SULT1A3
has Km ≈ 5 µM for dopamine, 4 µM for noradrenaline, 2 µM for adrenaline, and
Vmax on the order of 20–40 nmol/min/mg in liver cytosol (Coughtrie 2016; Pacifici
2004). It is the dominant sulfotransferase in the intestinal mucosa (>70% of
total SULT activity there), in platelets (the "M-type" classical assay), and in
brain catecholamine nuclei (locus coeruleus, substantia nigra). Hepatic SULT1A3
is low in adults (<10% of intestinal levels) — the gut is the primary first-pass
sulfation site for dietary tyramine and administered catecholamines.

Other relevant isoforms: SULT1A1 (phenolic xenobiotics, paracetamol —
Km ~150 µM, Vmax ~100 nmol/min/mg hepatic cytosol; Coughtrie 1998); SULT1E1
(oestrogens, Km 5 nM for E2); SULT2A1 (DHEA, bile acids, Km ~1 µM DHEA);
SULT1B1 (thyroid hormone conjugation). Paracetamol at therapeutic dose (≤3 g)
is sulfated primarily by SULT1A1 (55–65% of dose), with the rest going to
glucuronidation; at supratherapeutic dose (>5 g) SULT1A1 saturates and the
NAPQI-forming CYP2E1 pathway becomes proportionally larger — the biochemical
origin of paracetamol hepatotoxicity.

PAPS depletion and sulfate depletion are physiologically coupled to the SAM/SAH
methylation cycle. Cysteine, the sulfur source for PAPS, comes from methionine
via SAM → SAH → homocysteine → cystathionine (CBS, B6-dependent) → cysteine
(CSE/CTH) → sulfite (CDO1, cysteine dioxygenase) → sulfate (SUOX, sulfite
oxidase, molybdenum-dependent). Dietary methionine, cysteine, NAC, and
inorganic sulfate are all upstream inputs. Chronic catecholamine overflow, as
in sustained sympathetic stress, drains the PAPS pool because SULT1A3 runs at
high flux and SAM is simultaneously consumed for COMT-mediated O-methylation
(SAM → SAH). The SAM:SAH ratio drops, all SAM-dependent methylation
reactions slow down (DNA methylation, phospholipid PEMT, creatine synthesis),
and the system "hits a wall" when plasma sulfate drops below ~250 µM.

Supplementation kinetics: oral NAC (600–1800 mg) raises plasma cysteine by
~30% within 1–2 h but the conversion to sulfate via CDO1 is slow (t½ ≈ 6 h);
measurable plasma sulfate rise is 10–20 µM over baseline at 4–6 h post-dose.
Oral sodium thiosulfate (500 mg–5 g) raises plasma sulfate by 80–200 µM within
1–2 h but is limited by GI tolerability (>2 g produces diarrhoea in most
subjects). Intravenous thiosulfate (standard 12.5 g dose in cyanide antidote
protocols) raises plasma sulfate by 500–1000 µM and restores saturated SULT1A3
flux within 15 minutes. Cross-talk with glutathione synthesis is bidirectional:
NAC-derived cysteine is shared with GSH synthesis (rate-limited by γ-GCS), so
in high-oxidative-stress states NAC preferentially replenishes GSH before
feeding the CDO1 → sulfate branch.


## Section 5. GH–AMPK–mTORC1 oscillation

Adult growth-hormone (GH) secretion is pulsatile. Somatotropes in the anterior
pituitary fire bursts of 8–12 mIU/L peak amplitude every 2–4 h (male, awake) or
every 1–2 h (female, awake), driven by hypothalamic GHRH (stimulatory, arcuate
nucleus) and restrained by SST (somatostatin, inhibitory, periventricular
nucleus). Sleep adds a single large GH surge during early SWS. Each pulse lasts
15–30 min and is followed by a refractory period in which SST tone dominates
and GH falls to < 0.1 mIU/L. IGF-1, produced hepatically in response to GH,
feeds back on both the hypothalamus (increases SST) and the pituitary
(decreases GH) with a time-constant of ~20 h, which sets the slow envelope
of the daily GH pattern (Veldhuis, Roelfsema 2016).

At the cellular level, a GH pulse produces a metabolic biphasic response in
skeletal muscle and liver that couples to AMPK and mTORC1 in opposite phase
to each other — the "inhale / exhale" oscillation:

 AMPK INHALE phase (first 30–60 min post-pulse):
   - Lipolysis rises (GH → HSL → FFA), ATP demand for β-oxidation rises,
     AMP:ATP ratio transiently rises, LKB1 phosphorylates AMPK-α1/α2 on
     Thr172 and AMPK activity rises 3–5× (Hardie 2003, 2014).
   - AMPK phosphorylates TSC2 Ser1387, raising TSC2 GAP activity on Rheb →
     Rheb-GDP and mTORC1 is dephosphorylated at S2448 (Raptor-bound site).
   - AMPK also directly phosphorylates Raptor Ser792, creating a 14-3-3
     docking site that disassembles the active mTORC1 complex on the
     lysosomal surface (Gwinn et al., 2008, Mol Cell 30:214).
   - Cap-dependent translation suppressed; autophagy (ULK1 Ser555
     phosphorylation by AMPK) increased; catabolism primed.

 mTORC1 EXHALE phase (60–180 min post-pulse):
   - Hepatic IGF-1 rises, binds IGF-1R on myofiber, activates IR-IRS-1-PI3K
     signalling, Akt phosphorylates TSC2 Thr1462 (inhibitory) and PRAS40,
     disinhibiting mTORC1.
   - S6K1 Thr389 and 4E-BP1 Thr37/46 phosphorylation rise; cap-dependent
     translation surges; protein synthesis rate rises 40–80% over baseline
     for 60–90 min.
   - AMPK falls below baseline because ATP is replenished (SERCA, Na/K-ATPase
     restored) while demand drops.

In healthy adults, AMPK and mTORC1 anticorrelate strongly (r ≈ −0.7 in
Sabatini-group time-course data in C2C12 treated with pulsed IGF-1 /
simulated GH). Circadian phasing superimposes a slow envelope — AMPK runs
higher at night (catabolic/autophagic phase), mTORC1 higher during the day
(anabolic phase). In trenbolone-excess states the oscillator is biased: AR →
Src → PI3K → Akt bypasses the AMPK brake and mTORC1 remains high across both
phases, flattening the biphasic response. The S6K1-driven IRS-1 serine
phosphorylation loop (Section 1) then persists through what would normally
be the AMPK inhale phase, and the insulin-resistance phenotype locks in.

The vlPAG KATP channel is the autonomic echo of this energetic oscillation.
Kir6.2/SUR1 senses the local ADP/ATP ratio directly: when ATP drops (inhale
phase), SUR1 releases inhibition and KATP opens, hyperpolarising vlPAG and
permitting the freeze/quiescence switch. When ATP is replenished (exhale
phase), KATP closes and vlPAG fires. The dlPAG and vlPAG reciprocal switching
is therefore partly entrained to the systemic GH-AMPK-mTORC1 oscillation,
and chronic mTORC1 dominance that prevents AMPK flaring also prevents
vlPAG KATP opening — the parasympathetic-opioid arm of stress coping is
mechanically deprived of its molecular switch.


## Section 6. Astrocytic ECM mechanics and calcium waves

Astrocytic extracellular matrix (ECM) in the CNS is compositionally distinct
from peripheral connective tissue: it is dominated by hyaluronan, lecticans
(aggrecan, versican, neurocan, brevican), tenascin-R, and a thin collagen-IV
/ laminin basal lamina at the glia-limitans and perivascular endfoot. In
steady state the parenchymal ECM has a mechanical modulus of ~100–200 Pa
(comparable to very soft gels). Under chronic noradrenergic stress and
pro-fibrotic cytokine exposure (TGF-β, IL-6) lysyl oxidase (LOX) and LOXL2
expression in perivascular astrocytes rises, and they catalyse oxidative
deamination of lysine/hydroxylysine residues on collagen and elastin to
form allysine → pyridinoline cross-links. Cross-link density is the primary
determinant of tissue stiffness; accumulating LOX activity over weeks to
months can raise parenchymal modulus from ~200 Pa to ~600–800 Pa (Pogoda &
Janmey 2018; Sun 2019). That is still soft by peripheral standards but is a
3–4× change in the mechanical environment of astrocytes and neurons.

Astrocytes sense ECM stiffness through integrin-β1 / focal-adhesion-kinase
(FAK) / Rho-ROCK signalling into YAP/TAZ. On stiffer substrates, YAP/TAZ
translocate to the nucleus and drive transcription of ECM-remodelling genes
(CTGF, CYR61, FN1) and also ANKRD1, creating a positive feedback loop that
hardens the matrix further. Stiffness also reconfigures the astrocytic actin
cortex — F-actin bundles thicken and attach to the plasma membrane via
ezrin/radixin/moesin (ERM) proteins — which directly affects the gating of
mechanosensitive channels (PIEZO1, TRPV4) and the hemichannel pannexin-1
(Panx1).

Calcium waves are the principal long-range signalling mode of astrocyte
networks. They propagate via two mechanisms: (i) IP3 diffusion through
connexin-43 (Cx43) gap junctions → IP3R-gated ER Ca²⁺ release in the next
cell; and (ii) extracellular ATP release through Panx1 hemichannels and
Cx43 hemichannels → P2X7 / P2Y1 / P2Y2 receptor activation on neighbours →
Ca²⁺ influx or further IP3 rise. Panx1 opens in response to increased
intracellular Ca²⁺, Src-mediated phosphorylation of Panx1 Tyr198/308, and
mechanical stretch. P2X7 activation also feeds PKCθ (Section 2), providing a
mechanistic link between glial ATP release and peripheral insulin-signalling
serine-kinase activity in adjacent microvasculature.

Stiffer ECM attenuates calcium-wave propagation in two opposing ways. Short
waves (<100 µm) speed up because F-actin-mediated intracellular diffusion is
faster in a more tensioned cytoskeleton. Long-range waves (>300 µm) slow
down and become more fragmented because Cx43 gap junctions are down-regulated
under YAP/TAZ-driven ECM remodelling (Cx43 transcription is YAP-repressed in
reactive astrocytes) and because Panx1 hemichannel dwell time shortens under
high membrane tension. The net result is that a stiff glial matrix biases
the local network toward fast, short-range autocrine/paracrine ATP signalling
rather than long-range integrative Ca²⁺ waves.

The PAG is particularly sensitive to this stiffness bias. vlPAG glia wrap
neurons densely and contribute to the quiescence switch by releasing ATP
onto P2Y1 receptors on neighbouring GABAergic interneurons, which raises
local GABA tone. If the vlPAG ECM is stiffened by chronic LC-driven
noradrenergic tone, Panx1 ATP release drops, P2Y1-driven GABA tone weakens,
and the KATP/GIRK-mediated freeze switch becomes harder to engage — again
biasing the column selection away from the passive coping state and toward
sustained sympathetic lock-in.
